Why AI cannot be the System Engineer — and why that matters

Opening: "Let me start with a simple question. When a system fails, who is responsible?" [Pause] "Not which data was wrong. Not which model was incomplete. Not which AI gave a bad answer." [Pause] "Responsibility always points to a decision. And a decision only exists when uncertainty exists." "Today, AI systems are extremely good at producing answers. But answers are not decisions. And decisions cannot be automated without quietly removing responsibility."

From data to decision

Only decisions carry responsibility.
This is the foundational diagram. Everything else builds on this. Emphasize: this is not DIKW for academia — it's DIKW for engineering responsibility.

Why data is not knowledge

  • Data has no intrinsic meaning
  • Meaning arises from interpretation and validation
  • Context and intent are external to data
  • Data can be wrong, incomplete, or obsolete without "knowing" it
Example: A temperature reading (25°C) is data.
It becomes information when you know it's from Sensor #3 in Room 402.
It becomes knowledge when validated against expected range and declared fit-for-use.
It becomes a decision when you choose to act on it.
Use the temperature example to ground this. Emphasize: AI operates on data and information, not knowledge.

Why models are not knowledge

  • Models are abstractions with explicit assumptions
  • They encode simplifications and boundaries
  • They require interpretation, validation, and contextualization
  • A model's validity is always conditional

Models support knowledge — they do not replace it.

Critical point: Many AI systems are models. They don't "know" — they compute under assumptions.

Decision implies responsibility

  • No uncertainty → no decision (just execution)
  • No decision → no responsibility
  • Responsibility cannot be delegated to a tool
  • Automation does not eliminate decision — it displaces it
The biggest risk is not AI failure,
but unrecognized delegation of responsibility.
This is where certification concerns enter. Who signs? Who validates? Who assumes liability? AI cannot answer these questions.

Knowledge in Systems Engineering

Knowledge in SE is always:

  • Contextual — tied to system state, lifecycle phase, domain
  • Validated — reviewed, approved, baselined
  • Time-dependent — has validity periods, can become obsolete
  • Owned — someone is accountable for its correctness

Examples: requirements, architectures, interfaces, assumptions, margins, rationales

Connect to actual SE artifacts. This is not abstract — this is what engineers sign.

Why tacit knowledge cannot be "captured"

  • Tacit knowledge is context-sensitive and embodied
  • It emerges from experience, not from documentation
  • Attempts to formalize it often destroy its value
  • Experts "know more than they can tell"
Risk: LLMs appear to "have" tacit knowledge because they mimic expert expression.
This is dangerous — it's performance without understanding.
This is one of your strongest points. LLMs trained on expert text can sound expert — but they're not.

Knowledge Management ≠ Content Management

Knowledge Management is governance of:

  • Creation and capture
  • Validation and approval
  • Evolution and versioning
  • Obsolescence and archiving
  • Access and responsibility
Managing knowledge means managing
who is allowed to say "this is true enough to act on".
KM is not SharePoint or Confluence. It's governance of epistemic authority. AI can assist — but cannot govern.

What AI is and is not

AI: function, not agent

What AI is ✓

  • A computational system
  • Performing statistical inference
  • Trained on human-produced artifacts
  • Optimized for plausibility

What AI is not ✗

  • Not a cognitive subject
  • Not a knowledge owner
  • Not accountable
  • Not capable of intent or judgment
AI produces plausible answers, not accountable ones.
You're not attacking AI — you're placing it correctly. This prevents category errors.

AI as Virtual Assistant, not Virtual Engineer

A Virtual Assistant provides:

  • Retrieval and navigation
  • Synthesis and reformulation
  • Pattern suggestion
  • Support for exploration

But operates:

  • On representations, not reality
  • Without autonomy of purpose
  • Without responsibility
"Virtual Assistant" is the right abstraction. It's respectful to AI capabilities without over-claiming.

Families of AI by engineering function

Not "symbolic vs sub-symbolic"
but: what engineering problems they address

  1. Statistical / Predictive AI
  2. Optimization and Search AI
  3. Explainable AI (XAI)
  4. Generative AI (LLMs, multimodal)
Functional classification, not academic. Each family has strengths, weaknesses, and SE relevance.

1. Statistical / Predictive AI

Regression, classification, anomaly detection, forecasting

Strengths ✓

  • Good for pattern recognition
  • Measurable performance
  • Often verifiable statistically
  • Well-established methods

Weaknesses ✗

  • Context-blind
  • No semantic understanding
  • No explanation of why
  • Vulnerable to distribution shift

✓ SE relevance: Monitoring, diagnostics
✗ SE limitation: Decision justification

Classic ML — well understood, measurable. Good for technical monitoring, not for decisions.

2. Optimization and Search AI

Solvers, planners, constraint satisfaction, genetic algorithms

Strengths ✓

  • Excellent for trade studies
  • Explicit objective functions
  • Deterministic under assumptions
  • Can prove optimality

Weaknesses ✗

  • Depends on model correctness
  • Assumptions often hidden
  • Objective function choice is subjective
  • Can optimize the wrong thing perfectly

✓ SE relevance: Design exploration, trade-offs
✗ SE limitation: Responsibility for assumptions

Very useful in SE — but model quality is critical. Garbage in, optimized garbage out.

3. Explainable AI (XAI)

Rule-based systems, decision trees, symbolic reasoning, some causal models

Strengths ✓

  • Traceability of reasoning
  • Auditability
  • Easier V&V alignment
  • Can provide justifications

Weaknesses ✗

  • Limited scalability
  • Knowledge acquisition bottleneck
  • Often domain-specific
  • Maintenance burden

✓ SE relevance: Certification arguments, safety cases
✓ SE relevance: Critical decision support

This is the AI family closest to SE needs. Explainability is not optional for safety-critical systems.

XAI and ontologies

Explainable AI benefits significantly from ontological foundations:

  • Ontologies provide explicit semantics for reasoning rules
  • They enable semantic validation of explanations
  • They support cross-domain explanation transfer
  • They make assumptions explicit and auditable
Key insight: Ontologies don't compete with XAI — they ground it.
They define what is allowed to mean what.
This is where your ontology expertise shines. XAI without ontologies is just rule salad.

4. Generative AI (LLMs, multimodal)

Large Language Models, text generation, code generation, image synthesis

Strengths ✓

  • Navigate large knowledge spaces
  • Surface tacit knowledge traces
  • Natural interaction
  • Rapid synthesis

Weaknesses ✗

  • No semantic grounding
  • No truth commitment
  • Non-determinism
  • Hallucinations
  • Training opacity

✓ SE relevance: Exploration, documentation assistance
✗ SE limitation: Validation, certification, safety

This is where the hype lives — and the confusion. LLMs are powerful assistants, terrible authorities.

Why LLMs create epistemic confusion

  • They produce fluent, confident-sounding text
  • They mimic expert expression without expert understanding
  • They collapse data, knowledge, and judgment into plausible narrative
  • They make it easy to forget we need validation
When models disappear, hallucinations fill the gap.
This is the core problem with generative AI in SE. It's seductive — and that's dangerous.

Verification & Validation: the breaking point

Classical V&V assumes:

  • Determinism (same input → same output)
  • Explicit requirements
  • Testable acceptance criteria
  • Traceable design decisions

AI — especially generative AI — breaks all of these.

This is where AFIS people lean forward. V&V is foundational to SE — AI challenges it.

Why AI breaks classical V&V assumptions

  • Non-determinism — LLMs don't give the same answer twice
  • Training opacity — you can't inspect what was learned
  • Context drift — behavior changes with input distribution
  • No acceptance criteria — what does "good enough" mean?
  • Emergent behavior — capabilities not explicitly programmed
Each of these is a V&V showstopper in safety-critical contexts. You can't certify what you can't validate.

AI outputs as hypotheses, not facts

The only safe stance:

Treat all AI outputs as hypotheses requiring validation.

This means:

  • AI suggestions must be reviewed before use
  • Human validation is not optional
  • Traceability must capture who validated what
  • Responsibility remains with the human validator
This is the practical integration rule. AI accelerates hypothesis generation — humans validate.

V&V for different AI families

AI Family V&V Approach Certification Status
Statistical/Predictive Statistical validation, test sets, performance metrics Established methods exist
Optimization Verify model, validate objectives, test corner cases Possible with care
Explainable (XAI) Trace rules, audit logic, validate explanations Most certifiable
Generative (LLMs) Human review of each output Not certifiable directly

Certification readiness varies dramatically by AI type.

Responsibility cannot be automated

  • Automation shifts perceived responsibility
  • But legal and ethical responsibility remains human
  • AI creates a responsibility vacuum if not managed
  • "The AI decided" is never an acceptable answer
This is the ethical and legal core. Responsibility is not a technical property.

Critical questions for SE governance

When integrating AI, always ask:

  • Who decides? (not "what decides")
  • Who validates? (not "is it validated")
  • Who signs? (not "is it approved")
  • Who is liable? (not "is it safe")
These questions must have human names as answers.
These questions are your defense against automation narratives. If you can't name a person, you don't have responsibility.

A pragmatic integration pattern

How to actually integrate AI into SE practice:

  1. Explicit separation of artifacts
  2. Clear role boundaries
  3. Validation checkpoints
  4. Traceability of decisions
Move from principles to practice. Give them something they can implement Monday.

1. Explicit artifact separation

Maintain clear distinction between:

  • Baselined artifacts — validated, approved, controlled
    (requirements, models, specifications)
  • AI-generated suggestions — unvalidated, exploratory
    (drafts, alternatives, syntheses)
  • Human decisions — which suggestions to accept, reject, modify
    (with rationale and responsibility)

Never allow AI outputs to automatically become baselined artifacts.

2. Clear role boundaries

Activity AI Role Human Role
Requirements elicitation Suggest, reformulate Validate, approve
Architecture exploration Generate alternatives Select, justify
Document synthesis Draft, summarize Review, sign
Verification Suggest test cases Validate coverage
Trade studies Compute options Weight criteria, decide

3. Validation checkpoints

Insert explicit human validation before:

  • AI suggestion becomes requirement
  • AI draft becomes approved document
  • AI analysis becomes decision basis
  • AI output enters certification evidence

Each checkpoint must have:

  • Named validator
  • Validation criteria
  • Accept/reject decision
  • Documented rationale

4. Traceability of decisions

Record not just what was decided, but:

  • Who decided
  • When they decided
  • Why they decided (rationale)
  • What alternatives were considered
  • What uncertainties remain
If AI was involved, trace:
what AI suggested → what human decided → why

What AFIS working groups should watch for

Be alert to these dangerous narratives:

This section helps people resist marketing pressure. Give them language to push back.

Dangerous narrative #1

"AI understands the system"

Reality:

  • AI manipulates representations, not meanings
  • It has no model of intent or purpose
  • It cannot distinguish important from unimportant
  • "Understanding" requires grounding AI lacks
This is the most common and dangerous claim. Understanding requires intentionality.

Dangerous narrative #2

"We'll just validate the AI outputs"

Reality:

  • If you can validate everything, you don't need AI
  • If you can't validate everything, you're accepting risk
  • Validation cost may exceed generation savings
  • Non-determinism makes comprehensive testing impossible
This reveals the validation paradox. "Just validate" is not a strategy.

Dangerous narrative #3

"Knowledge graphs solve the grounding problem"

Reality:

  • Knowledge graphs are valuable infrastructure
  • But they don't make AI "understand"
  • Symbol grounding remains unsolved
  • Responsibility still requires human judgment
Knowledge graphs help — but they're not magic. They don't eliminate the need for human judgment.

Dangerous narrative #4

"Autonomous engineering systems"

Reality:

  • Autonomy implies independent decision-making
  • Independent decisions require accountability
  • AI systems cannot be held accountable
  • Therefore "autonomous engineering" is a category error
"Autonomous" is marketing language. It confuses automation with autonomy.

Better questions to ask

Instead of accepting narratives, ask:

  • Who validates this AI output?
  • Who decides whether to act on it?
  • Who signs the safety case?
  • What happens when the AI is wrong?
  • How do we trace this decision in 5 years?
  • Can this be certified?

If these questions make people uncomfortable,
you're asking the right questions.

Common objections and responses

"But AI can reason now"

Response:

  • AI performs inference over representations
  • This is computation, not reasoning in the SE sense
  • Reasoning in SE requires understanding purpose and consequences
  • AI has neither — it pattern-matches

"This will slow us down"

Response:

  • Validation always takes time — AI doesn't change that
  • Fast but wrong is not an improvement
  • Clear boundaries actually speed integration
  • Confusion and rework are what slow projects

"Other companies are moving faster"

Response:

  • Speed without rigor leads to failure
  • We integrate AI where it adds value
  • We maintain responsibility where it's required
  • This is competitive advantage, not obstacle
